    Is my component compatible?

    • check the voltage jumper on the psoc, can operate at 5 or 3.3V
    • Input in general
      • Voltage is generally the concern, not current
      • this is because GPIO pins are "high impedance", which means that they are effectively a very high internal resistance, taking little current to sample a signal.
      • ADCs are digitizing the range of voltage they are sampling
        • 8-bits - 255 discrete numbers. at 5V, that's approximately .02V per division
        • if you have a small range of input values,
    • Analog Input
      • Anything analog input is compatible because it is your job to signal-condition it
      • we are teaching op-amps, but you could use transistors, vacuum tubes, many other technologies
    • Digital Input
      • switches - explain pullup or pulldown concept, why you need them
      • explain PSoC4 weak pull up configuration
      • what happens when you have a digital device operating at a higher voltage than expected?
    • Output
      • Need to consider current output capabilities
        • if you are trying to draw more current than possible, you will either will destroy your microcontroller, or not be able to supply the desired current, depending on its design
      • Look at the data sheet
        • absolute maximum ratings
        • find the PSoC datasheet: https://www.infineon.com/dgdl/Infineon-PSoC_4000T_MCU_datasheet-DataSheet-v09_00-EN.pdf?fileId=8ac78c8c8a44f57b018a799f5fb53b5d
        • about 25 mA
      • Draw a microcontroller straight to an LED
        • what's wrong?
        • no current limiting resistor for LED
        • no current limit for microcontroller.
      • Draw a microcontroller straight to a motor
        • can't run a motor straight from a microcontroller.
        • Fix the drawing by adding a mosfet
    • What's the difference between a BJT and a MOSFET?
      • BJT: current controlled current amplifier
        • limited amplification ability per stage.
      • MOSFET: voltage controlled resistance change.
        • mosfets take a larger voltage to fully turn on, so bad for low-voltage applications
